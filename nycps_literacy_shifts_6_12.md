Though there are many factors that may impact our students' educational trajectory, one factor firmly in our locus of control is teaching all students, including multilingual learners and students with disabilities, to read and write proficiently. The data makes a clear case for why we, as educators, must shift to research-based approaches to ensure all of our students are getting the skills they need to graduate from high school prepared for college and careers.   To improve our students’ literacy outcomes, it is imperative that we explicitly teach the various components that create skilled readers and writers. To this end, our instructional shifts are anchored in the science of reading and evidence-based writing practices. Understanding and integrating these evidence-based approaches not only aligns with our dedication to academic excellence, but also ensures that educators are equipped with the most current and effective strategies for fostering literacy skills in our diverse student body. What is the Science of Reading? The science of reading refers to a comprehensive body of research completed over many years by experts in the fields of education, psychology, neuroscience, language development, and more. Recent advances in technology and a greater understanding of neurobiology have allowed researchers and practitioners who work with typical and struggling readers to understand how reading develops in the brain and the skills that contribute to proficient reading. This empirically-supported research provides us with the information we need to gain a deeper understanding of how we learn to read, what skills are involved, how they work together, and which parts of the brain are responsible for reading development (Gentry & Ouellete, 2019; Petscher et al., 2020; The Reading League, 2022). The science of reading is not an ideology or philosophy, a one-size-fits-all approach; a specific program of instruction, or a single component of instruction, such as phonics. Furthermore, the science of reading does not discount the importance of motivation and engagement, which play a crucial role for adolescent learners (Kamil et al., 2008). As stated in  Reading Next , "a lack of incentive and engagement…explains why even skilled readers and writers often do not progress in reading and academic achievement in middle and high schools” (Biancarosa & Snow, 2006). While the science of reading underscores the mechanics of literacy, it equally recognizes the pivotal roles of motivation and engagement, especially during the critical learning phases of secondary school. Without these elements, the journey toward literacy and academic success, even for those with foundational skills, may falter in the face of dwindling student interest.   Instruction aligned to the science of reading is informed by two models: The Simple View of Reading (Gough & Tunmer, 1986) and the Reading Rope (Scarborough, 2001). The Simple View emphasizes that strong reading comprehension cannot occur unless both decoding (word recognition) and language comprehension (vocabulary and syntactic awareness) are developed. The Reading Rope builds on this by visually identifying eight components that work together like strands in a rope to enable skilled reading. They are organized into language comprehension and word recognition categories, similar to the Simple View. The strands in the rope become more tightly intertwined as students become increasingly more automatic and strategic in their ability to apply all of the components.   3

The science of reading has established that explicit instruction is associated with beneficial outcomes for students. Explicit instruction is a structured and systematic teaching approach that provides clear design, instruction and delivery procedures. This approach includes modeling new skills with explicit explanations, giving students ample practice with feedback, and providing opportunities for review and distributed practice. The goal of explicit instruction is to foster independent mastery of reading and writing skills (Archer & Hughes, 2010; Fletcher et al., 2019; Vaughn & Fletcher, 2021).   Effective literacy instruction in secondary school necessitates comprehensive strategies that enhance both comprehension and word recognition skills across all subjects. Research on adolescent reading instruction supports the teaching of skills related to the language comprehension part of the Reading Rope to all students. All secondary students should receive explicit vocabulary and comprehension strategy instruction and opportunities for extended discussion of text meaning and interpretation in all subjects (Kamil et al., 2008). In addition, students should regularly write about reading to support comprehension and content learning (Graham & Hebert, 2010). A body of research also exists that informs evidence-based writing instruction practices (Graham & Perin, 2007; Graham & Hebert, 2010; Graham et al. 2017). This includes explicit teaching of writing strategies for planning, revising and editing; frequent writing for varied purposes; building fluency in spelling and sentence skills; collaborative writing; and the study of models. Research also emphasizes the importance of using writing to support reading, including teaching students how to respond to a text in writing, writing notes about and summaries of a text, and answering or creating questions in writing about a text (Graham & Hebert, 2010). 4

Research also indicates that older students who have difficulty reading benefit from explicit instruction and targeted intervention in word recognition skills if they have not developed automaticity with word recognition (Vaughn et al., 2022). Students with reading difficulties who do not receive interventions tailored to their needs will not be able to meaningfully engage with high- quality, grade-level text. Thus, it is essential that educational strategies for middle and high school students integrate targeted interventions and cross-content literacy practices to ensure that all students, particularly those facing reading challenges, gain the necessary skills to understand and engage with complex, grade-level texts. American Federation of Teachers President Randi Weingarten says it beautifully: “Ultimately, the Science of Reading is inextricably linked to the love of reading. To teach and inspire the next generation, we simply can’t have one without the other.” Therefore, in addition to the six literacy shifts named here, schools and classrooms should also maintain robust libraries and library systems, find space and time for student choice, celebrate students' reading and writing accomplishments, and partner with families on reading and writing routines that can be implemented at home. 5

Just as students face increasingly complex literacy demands, the amount of time they spend engaged with such texts tends to diminish. In typical middle schools, students are allocated a 45-minute English Language Arts period. However, it is imperative for students to engage daily with texts for at least two hours - ideally four hours (Biancarosa & Snow, 2006). Data sourced from NYCPS’s Secondary Literacy Pilot (2010) and the Middle School Quality Initiative (2011-13) revealed that students often spent as little as 10-20 minutes interacting with texts per day. Indeed, “researchers have found that some teachers circumvent the need for students to read texts by adjusting their assignments or methods of presenting content, rather than helping students learn the strategies needed for content- area work” (Kamil et al., 2008). Consequently, a situation arises where although students are provided access to the content, those who require the most practice with texts receive the least opportunity. While some of the time students spend engaging with text will be in the English Language Arts classroom, it is equally essential that students spend extended time engaging with text in subjects like Science and History. This instruction should be text- centered and guided by instructional principles designed to convey both content and the improvement and practice of literacy skills (Biancarosa & Snow, 2006). Although reading strategies might be taught explicitly in ELA or strategic reading classes, students are unlikely to generalize them unless teachers also explicitly teach these strategies in content- area classes (Crawford & Torgesen, 2007). Cross-content literacy instruction should use research-based, high-leverage before, during and after reading routines. Similar cross- content routines ensure that students are engaged in learning content rather than trying to understand the different tools teachers use to aid in comprehension.
